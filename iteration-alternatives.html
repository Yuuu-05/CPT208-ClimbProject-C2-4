Prototype Evolution

The UI direction changed across three major stages: from a simple early interface, to a richer sports-app direction, and finally to a simplified accessibility-focused Hi-Fi prototype.

Stage 1

Stage 1 — Early UI Exploration

What it explored

  • A simple mobile interface structure.
  • Basic settings and early navigation.
  • A first attempt to make the climbing assistant approachable.

What worked

  • The interface was visually simple.
  • The main actions were easy to identify.

What it lacked

  • The design was still too basic for a complete climbing support workflow.
  • Accessibility needs were not yet strongly reflected.
  • Scan guidance had not yet become the clear center of the experience.
Early UI Design
Stage 2

Stage 2 — Expanded Sports-App Direction

What changed

  • The visual direction shifted to a green climbing / sports-app style.
  • More functions were added to make the product feel complete.
  • The design explored dashboard, route support, guidance, and richer app-like features.

Why we expanded it

  • We wanted to test whether a fuller sports-app structure would make the system feel more realistic.
  • We explored how HoldLight could support more than one climbing task.

What we learned

  • The product felt more complete, but also became too feature-heavy.
  • The richer structure risked increasing cognitive load for blind and low-vision users.
  • This pushed us to focus on clearer accessibility support instead of feature density.

Latest Hi-Fi Direction

Stage 3 — Accessibility-Focused Hi-Fi Prototype

What changed

  • We kept the mature green visual identity from the middle version.
  • We removed unnecessary complexity.
  • The flow became more scan-first and task-focused.
  • The interface focused on route scanning, route confirmation, short guidance, and accessibility support.

Why we simplified it

  • Blind and low-vision users need clarity, not feature density.
  • During climbing, users should not process too many options at once.
  • A simpler scan-first flow better supports safety, confidence, and low cognitive load.

Why this became the final direction

  • It best matches our user requirements.
  • It makes the main task easier to start.
  • It supports audio guidance and fallback more clearly.
  • It is more aligned with human-centered accessibility design.

Why the Design Changed

Shift 01

From simple to complete

The first version helped us test basic structure, but it did not yet support the full route guidance workflow. This led us to explore a richer app-like direction.

Shift 02

From complete to complex

The middle version added more features and looked more like a sports app, but it also created higher visual and interaction complexity.

Shift 03

From complex to focused

The final version reduced unnecessary functions and focused on the most important accessibility task: helping users scan, understand, and follow a climbing route with less cognitive load.

Key iteration insight:

Our design evolved from “more features” to “clearer support”. The final prototype prioritizes accessibility, scan-first interaction, and low-overload guidance over feature density.
